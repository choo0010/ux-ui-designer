Auto Nest

A mobile app that brings the entire car-buying journey into one place — browse listings, compare vehicles, and find your dream car without bouncing between a dozen different websites.

Role UX Designer · UI Designer
Tools Figma · Adobe Illustrator
Type Academic Project · Algonquin College IMD
Deliverables User Research · Wireframes · Hi-fi Figma Screens · Clickable Prototype

The problem

Buying a car is one of the biggest purchases most people make — yet the experience of finding one is fragmented and frustrating. Buyers move between multiple websites to compare listings, check specs, read reviews, and contact sellers. There's no single, well-designed mobile experience that brings all of that together. The result is decision fatigue, missed deals, and a process that feels overwhelming rather than exciting.

The goal

Design a mobile app that consolidates car discovery, comparison, and purchase enquiry into one streamlined experience. Auto Nest needed to feel like it was on the buyer's side — reducing the noise, surfacing the right vehicles, and making it genuinely enjoyable to find your next car.

01
User research & surveys

Conducted surveys to understand how people currently search for cars — what frustrates them, what platforms they use, and where the biggest drop-off moments are in the buying journey.

02
User flows & IA

Mapped the core journeys: searching and filtering listings, saving favourites, comparing two vehicles side by side, and reaching out to a seller — defining the app's full information architecture.

03
Wireframes

Built low-fidelity wireframes for every key screen — home, search results, vehicle detail, comparison view, and saved listings — iterating on layout and navigation before any visual design.

04
Hi-fi UI & prototype

Designed high-fidelity screens in Figma with a premium dark visual language, then built a fully clickable prototype covering the end-to-end buying journey.

Design decisions

The dark, gradient-rich visual style was a deliberate choice — cars carry associations of performance, aspiration, and technology. A bright, clinical UI would have undermined the premium feeling the app needed. The splash screen leads with the car as the hero visual and the tagline "Find your dream car," which immediately anchors the user emotionally before they've interacted with a single feature.

The search and filter system was the most complex UX challenge — car buyers have very specific requirements (make, model, year, price, mileage, location) but presenting all of those filters at once is overwhelming. I solved this by using a progressive disclosure pattern: a simple search bar upfront, with advanced filters available one tap away, revealed only when the user needs them.

Auto Nest — onboarding and home screen. Designed in Figma with a fully clickable prototype.

What I learned

The research phase genuinely changed my design direction. I went in assuming the main problem was a lack of listings — but the surveys revealed that the real frustration was too many listings with no intelligent filtering. People weren't struggling to find cars; they were struggling to narrow down the options. That insight shifted the entire focus of the app from "show more" to "show less, but better." It was my first real experience of research overriding a designer's assumption — and it made the final product significantly stronger.

Research-led design

User surveys conducted upfront — insights directly shaped the app's core feature priority.

Full Figma prototype

Clickable prototype covering the complete car discovery and enquiry journey end-to-end.

Progressive filtering

Complex search reduced to a clean, two-step pattern — simple by default, powerful when needed.

MCD Crew App

A mobile app that gives McDonald's crew members one place to check schedules, receive task assignments, and stay connected with their team — replacing group chats and paper rosters with a single, purpose-built tool.

Role UX Designer · UI Designer
Tools Figma · Adobe Illustrator
Type Academic Project · Algonquin College IMD
Deliverables User Research · Wireframes · Hi-fi Figma Screens · Clickable Prototype

The problem

McDonald's crew members — many of them young, first-time workers — manage their work lives through a patchwork of tools: schedules sent by text, tasks communicated verbally during handovers, and team announcements buried in personal WhatsApp group chats. There's no single place to check when you're working, what you need to do, or what your manager needs from you. This scattered approach leads to missed shifts, forgotten tasks, and communication breakdowns during the busiest hours of service.

The goal

Design a crew-facing mobile app with three core jobs: let staff check their schedules instantly, receive and track task assignments from managers, and stay connected with team announcements — all without leaving the app. The experience had to be fast enough to use in a two-minute break and simple enough that a new crew member could pick it up on their first shift with zero training.

01
User research & surveys

Surveyed and interviewed fast food crew members about their current workflows — what tools they use, where things go wrong, and what information they need most urgently before and during a shift.

02
User flows & IA

Defined three core pillars — My Schedule, My Tasks, and Team Feed — and mapped every user flow: checking upcoming shifts, marking a task complete, and reading manager announcements.

03
Wireframes

Low-fidelity wireframes for all key screens, tested with peers for speed and clarity. Iterated on the home screen three times before landing on a layout that surfaces the most urgent information immediately.

04
Hi-fi UI & prototype

Final high-fidelity screens in Figma using McDonald's brand colours, connected into a fully clickable prototype that demonstrates all three core journeys end-to-end.

Design decisions

Using McDonald's golden yellow and near-black as the primary palette was a deliberate choice grounded in the research. Crew members already operate inside that brand environment every day — the uniform, the packaging, the in-store signage. A familiar visual language meant zero cognitive load when picking up a new tool. The app should feel like it belongs, not like something imported from outside.

The schedule view is the home screen — not a dashboard, not a feed. Research made clear that the first question every crew member asks before a shift is "When do I work next, and what station am I on?" That question gets answered the moment the app opens. Tasks and the team feed are one tap away, but they don't compete with the schedule for attention. Hierarchy was everything.

Typography throughout is large, bold, and high contrast. The app gets used quickly — often standing up, in a noisy kitchen, with one eye on the fryer. Every piece of critical information needed to land in under two seconds.

MCD Crew App — schedule view. The home screen answers the crew member's most important question the moment the app opens.

What I learned

This project pushed back hardest on one of my instincts as a designer: adding features feels like adding value. The research said otherwise. Crew members during a five-minute break don't want a rich feature set — they want three things fast: their shift, their tasks, their messages. Every feature I considered adding went through the same filter: does this help a crew member do their job better, or does it just make the app feel more complete to me as a designer? Most ideas didn't survive that question. The best version of this app is a focused one.

I also learned how powerful it is to design within a brand system that users already trust. Leaning into McDonald's visual language wasn't a constraint — it was a shortcut to familiarity that made the whole experience feel more credible from the first screen.

Schedules, tasks & team

All three crew needs solved in one app — no more juggling texts, paper rosters, and group chats.

Zero-training usability

Designed to be picked up and used correctly on a first shift, with no onboarding required.

End-to-end prototype

Fully clickable Figma prototype demonstrating all three core crew journeys from open to completion.
